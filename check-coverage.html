Check Coverage

Healthcare Express Scripts

Check Coverage began as a compliance tool required to meet Transparency in Coverage regulations. Over several years, I led its evolution into a member-first, decision-support product — helping people confidently understand medication coverage, compare prices, and choose how and where to fill their prescriptions.

Challenge

Availability of information did not equal clarity or confidence. Members oriented first to price, used coverage details to interpret why that price looked the way it did, and lost trust when changes or limitations weren't clearly explained.

Objectives

Guide the experience from disclosure-focused transparency toward clear, explainable, comparison-driven decision support — using research, analytics, and iterative design to align the product with real member needs.

Role & Tools

UX Design Advisor — multi-year engagement (2022–2025) spanning moderated usability testing, qualitative interviews, concept and language testing, call-reason exploration, analytics and behavioral data, and literature reviews on medication information-seeking behavior.

Starting Point

The Starting Point (2022): Transparency as Compliance

Check Coverage first launched in 2022 to satisfy Transparency in Coverage requirements. At that time, success was defined by whether information was available and accurate — not whether it was understandable or actionable.

Early characteristics of the experience included:

  • Information structured around internal benefit logic
  • Heavy reliance on insurance terminology
  • Limited support for comparison or decision-making

While the tool met regulatory goals, early research and analytics made it clear that availability of information did not equal clarity or confidence.

Product Shift

The Product Shift: Replacing "Price a Medication"

As both tools matured, the long-term direction became clear: Check Coverage needed to replace the legacy "Price a Medication" tool and become the primary destination for medication pricing and coverage questions.

This shift raised expectations significantly. To succeed, Check Coverage had to:

  • Support real comparison, not just answers
  • Reduce confusion for members with low insurance literacy
  • Explain pricing and coverage decisions clearly
  • Scale beyond compliance into a durable, trusted product

Research Foundation

A Multi-Year Research Foundation

This work was informed by continuous research from 2022 through 2025, including moderated usability testing, qualitative interviews, concept and language testing, call-reason exploration, analytics and behavioral data, and literature reviews on medication information-seeking behavior.

Rather than treating research as one-off validation, I used it as a cumulative input. Each study sharpened our understanding of when, why, and how members think about medication cost and coverage.

Research Findings

What Stayed Consistent Across the Research

Finding 01

Price is the Primary Signal

Members orient first to price, then use coverage to explain why it looks that way. Coverage without price context feels incomplete; price without explanation feels untrustworthy.

Finding 02

Language Shapes Confidence More Than Precision

Members preferred clear, familiar language — "Not covered" over "Outside plan," "In network" over "Recommended." Emotional clarity mattered more than technical precision.

Finding 03

Comparison Is the Core Job to Be Done

Members bypassed category framing and went straight to viewing all prices. This reinforced a shift toward e-commerce-inspired comparison patterns over step-by-step decision funnels.

Finding 04

Confidence Breaks During Change

Confusion spikes during new prescriptions, brand → generic switches, deductible resets, pharmacy changes. The goal became preventing surprise — not eliminating calls entirely.

Finding 05

There Is a Narrow Window of Usefulness

Check Coverage is most valuable after a medication is discussed but before reaching the pharmacy counter. This shaped entry points, messaging, and information hierarchy.

Members approach medication pricing the way they approach shopping — they want to compare options, understand tradeoffs, and know what to do next.

Design Strategy

Design Strategy: From Competitors to Commerce

Early competitive analysis revealed that many healthcare tools shared the same issues: dense language, limited comparison, and unclear pricing states. I helped shift the strategy toward familiar e-commerce patterns, based on a simple insight: members approach medication pricing the way they approach shopping.

This reframing influenced side-by-side comparisons, progressive disclosure instead of front-loaded rules, and clear defaults with optional customization.

Analytics

Analytics Over Time (and the UX Enhancements They Informed)

Research told us why members struggled. Analytics showed us where that struggle appeared in the experience — and what members tried to do about it. Over time, I paired usability findings with a longitudinal analytics view that tracked how people actually moved through Check Coverage as the UI evolved.

The story the funnel told: Check Coverage attracted seven-figure traffic across its entry points. Most members who landed were not browsing — they were looking for answers. More than four out of five progressed into results once a medication search began, and roughly seventy percent continued into medication detail. Where the story changed was in the middle. Analytics consistently showed increased drop-off at mid-flow transitions — especially when an interstitial appeared before results. The interruption was slowing people down and in some cases preventing them from seeing full pharmacy comparisons before making a decision.

How members tried to help themselves: Almost all searches were by medication name, not NDC — confirming that people approached the tool with everyday language, not benefit codes. A meaningful portion of users opened FAQ content, especially around plan rules and prices and paying, signaling moments where reassurance mattered more than speed. The brand ↔ generic toggle saw heavy use.

Turning patterns into UX decisions: The analytics told a consistent story: members were behaving like shoppers in a system still optimized for disclosure. That story directly informed several UX enhancements — reducing restart cost by adding edit-in-place controls on results, treating "price unavailable" as a trust moment requiring explanation and next steps, and leaning fully into comparison with side-by-side layouts and visible price hierarchy.

Design Principles

Research-Informed Design Principles

01

Lead with cost, support with explanation

Price should be visible early, paired with coverage context and clear explanations. Members should not have to hunt for the number that matters most.

02

Use familiar language, then explain nuance

Start with language members recognize — plain, everyday words. Offer depth and detail when they need it, not upfront.

03

Treat comparison as the primary experience

Comparison is not a feature — it is the job to be done. The interface should make side-by-side evaluation the default, not an afterthought.

04

Make change explicit

When something differs from expectations, name it and explain why. Silence around change is the source of most member confusion.

05

Design for confidence, not just task completion

Success means members leave understanding what they decided and why — not just that they completed a flow.

2025

2025: Market Commitments as an Inflection Point

In 2025, Evernorth publicly committed to a new era of pharmacy benefit services, emphasizing upfront rebate-free pricing, greater transparency and simplicity, lower out-of-pocket costs, and stronger support for local and community pharmacies.

These commitments validated what research had already shown: transparency must be predictable, explainable, and trustworthy at the moment of decision. From a UX perspective, this raised the bar — pricing needed to feel immediate and explainable, "price unavailable" could no longer be a dead end, coverage decisions needed clearer rationale, and pharmacy choice needed to reflect real access and convenience.

Reflection

Reflection

This work reinforced that designing in regulated spaces doesn't mean designing defensively. The real challenge — and opportunity — is translating complex systems into experiences people can actually use.

The most impactful decisions weren't visual; they were strategic: treating pricing like shopping not billing, treating transparency as understanding not disclosure, and treating trust as something the interface must actively earn.

Check Coverage wasn't redesigned once — it was continuously shaped by real member behavior over time. That continuity of learning is what ultimately allowed the product to mature from a compliance obligation into a decision-support experience members can rely on.
